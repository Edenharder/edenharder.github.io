In their inspiring paper, Li-Yorke give a definition of chaos and prove the Period Three Theorem which says

Let \(J\) be an an interval and let \(F: J \to J\) be continuous. Assume there is a point \(a \in J\) for which the points \(b = F(a), c = F^2(a)\) and \(d = F^3(a)\), satisfy \[ d \leq a < b < c ~\text{or}~ d \geq a > b > c. \] Then

  1. for every \(k = 1, 2, \cdots\) there is a periodic point in \(J\) having period \(k\).
  2. Furthermore, there is an uncountable set \(S \subset J\) (containing no periodic points), which satisfies the following conditions: For every \(p, q \in S\) with \(p \neq q\), \[ \lim\sup_{n\to \infty} |F^n(p) - F^n(q)| > 0, \lim\inf_{n\to \infty} |F^n(p) - F^n(q)| = 0. \] For every \(p \in S\) and periodic point \(q \in J\), \[ \lim\sup_{n\to \infty} |F^n(p) - F^n(q)| > 0. \]

REMARKS:

  1. The conclusion is the Li-Yorke’s definition of chaos.
  2. Notice that if there is a periodic point with period 3, then the hypothesis of the theorem will be satisfied.
  3. Freeman Dyson approves this paper in his article birds and frogs: The Li-Yorke paper is one of the immortal gems in the literature of mathematics.
